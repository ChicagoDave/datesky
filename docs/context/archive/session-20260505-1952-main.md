### 1. Mobile Navigation Overhaul

The top nav was overflowing and wrapping on mobile because six or more items competed for space (About, Browse, My Profile, Settings, Log out, @handle). A hamburger menu was explicitly rejected. Replaced with a bottom tab bar — the dominant pattern on mobile-first dating products.

**New `src/components/MobileTabBar.tsx`.** Three tabs: Browse, Profile, Settings. Sticky to the viewport bottom on mobile (`md:hidden`). Auth-gated (renders nothing when unauthenticated). Active tab indicated by a brand-gradient top border (`bg-gradient-to-r from-[#d60270] via-[#9b4f96] to-[#0038a8]`). Active state detected via `usePathname()`. iOS safe-area inset honored via `pb-[env(safe-area-inset-bottom)]`.

## Key Decisions

### 1. Three-tab bar, not four or more

Browse, Profile, and Settings are the three actions a logged-in user takes repeatedly. About is reference content — it belongs in the top nav where it is visible to unauthenticated visitors as well. Log out is destructive and infrequent — it belongs in Settings, not at thumb-reach in a tab bar. Keeping the bar to three tabs avoids icon-label crowding on small screens.

### 2. Log out moves to /settings, not a nav dropdown

A dropdown or sheet on the top nav would require additional interaction state and still competes with the limited mobile header real estate. The Settings page is the natural home for account-management actions. Users accustomed to mobile apps expect to find Log out there.

### 3. Nav split by visibility class, not separate components

The mobile and desktop right-side nav are two `div` elements inside the same `Nav` component, toggled with `flex md:hidden` and `hidden md:flex`. A single component is easier to reason about and avoids prop-drilling or context just to share the session/user handle.
